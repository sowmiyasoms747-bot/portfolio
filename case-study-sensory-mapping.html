Sensory-Friendly Routing

Shifting navigation from a speed-focused system to a comfort-driven experience, prioritizing predictability, accessibility, and emotional well-being throughout the journey.

Overview

What is Sensory-Friendly Routing?

This project introduces a Sensory-Friendly Routing feature added directly into Google Maps. It helps neurodiverse users — including people with autism, sensory processing disorder, anxiety, and mobility challenges — navigate their daily journeys without sensory overload. Instead of routing based only on speed and distance, this feature considers noise levels, crowd density, rest stop availability, and lighting to find the calmest path possible.

Why I Chose This

I chose this problem because 1 in 7 people globally have some form of disability, yet no major navigation app today considers their sensory needs. Google Maps tells you the fastest route. But for millions of neurodiverse users, the fastest route is rarely the right route. I wanted to design something that genuinely makes independent travel possible for people who are currently left out.

The Goal

My goal is to make everyday navigation calmer, more predictable, and genuinely accessible for neurodiverse users — without making them feel labelled or different from anyone else.

Key Discovery

Neurodiverse users spend more energy planning the journey than taking it. The stress starts before they even leave home.

The Strategy

What I Decided to Solve

I introduced a Sensory Mode feature addition to Google Maps that gives neurodiverse users the ability to set their personal sensory preferences — noise, crowds, lighting, rest stops, and restroom access — and receive routes scored for comfort, not just speed. An AI layer learns preferences over time and provides real-time advance alerts during navigation so users are never caught off guard.

Walkthrough

Interactive Screen Walkthrough

01

Entry Point (Sensory Toggle)

A new “Sensory” option is introduced directly within the directions interface of Google Maps. Placing the feature here ensures discoverability at the moment users are actively planning a journey, rather than hiding it inside settings.

02

Sensory Preferences

Users can quickly personalize their experience by selecting sensitivity levels for noise, crowd density, accessibility, and rest stops. The design keeps interactions simple and optional, reducing cognitive load while still allowing meaningful customization.

03

Route Selection (Sensory Scoring)

Routes are presented with a sensory score alongside time and distance, allowing users to balance comfort and efficiency. Instead of labeling routes, a neutral scoring system makes the feature inclusive and accessible to all users.

04

Map with Environmental Overlays

The map view is enhanced with visual overlays indicating quiet zones, busy areas, and available rest points. This helps users build a mental model of their journey and reduces uncertainty before and during navigation.

05

Navigation with Smart Alerts

During navigation, the system provides proactive alerts about upcoming crowded or noisy areas. Users are offered optional detours, ensuring they remain in control while being supported by real-time insights.

Problem Space & Solutions

Real Problems. Smart Solutions.

The Challenges

P1

Google Maps routes only by speed and distance, ignoring sensory needs.

P2

No advance warning when a noisy or crowded area is coming up.

P3

Rest stops and restrooms are not shown along a walking route.

P4

Neurodiverse users have to use 3 or more apps just to plan one safe journey.

P5

Unexpected changes mid-journey cause real anxiety and distress.

P6

No navigation app today learns and adapts to an individual's sensory profile.

The Interventions

S1

Added sensory scoring to every route showing comfort level out of 10.

S2

AI gives advance alerts at least 300 metres before a disturbance.

S3

Rest stops and accessible restrooms are mapped and pinned along the route.

S4

One single feature inside Google Maps handles everything.

S5

Calm mode gives a one-tap safe exit during overwhelming moments.

S6

AI learns preferences over time and auto-fills them before every trip.

Reflection

What I Learned

01

Predictability is a functional need, not a preference

For neurodiverse users, uncertainty is a real barrier to travel, making predictable routes an absolute necessity.

02

Edge cases are the most important design work

The calm mode SOS button taught me that designing for extreme stress moments often defines the core product utility.

03

AI should be invisible

The best AI experience is when users never actively think about the algorithms, but simply notice the app keeps matching their requirements better over time.

04

Entry points matter as much as features

Moving the sensory toggle to the active directions page instead of hiding it in deep settings completely changed its discoverability and rate of use.

05

Designing for the underserved helps everyone

Designing for one specific underserved group almost always ends up improving the universal usability and experience for all users.
